HIGH RISK

üìû Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Attackers call pretending to be "IT Support," "The Bank," or even law enforcement.

üö® New Threat: AI Voice Cloning
Attackers can now clone a CEO's or family member's voice using just 3 seconds of audio from social media or public recordings.
Real Example: "Hi, this is Mark from IT. We detected suspicious activity on your account. I need you to verify your password to secure it."
EMERGING

üì± Quishing (QR Phishing)

Malicious QR codes placed on parking meters, restaurant menus, or fake flyers.

üö® Tactic: Stickers are placed over legitimate codes. Scanning takes you to a fake payment site to steal credit card info.
Real Example: A fake QR code on a parking meter redirects to a convincing clone of the city's parking payment site.
CRITICAL

üîî MFA Fatigue

Spamming your phone with "Approve Login" notifications, often at inconvenient times like 2 AM.

üö® Defense: Never approve a request you didn't initiate. If they call claiming it's a "glitch," hang up immediately.
Real Example: Uber breach (2022) - Attacker spammed employee with MFA requests, then called pretending to be IT to get approval.
COMMON

üí¨ Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Fake text messages claiming to be from delivery services, banks, or government agencies.

üö® Red Flag: Messages with urgent requests and shortened links (bit.ly, tinyurl) are almost always malicious.
Real Example: "Your package delivery failed. Update address: [malicious link]"
SOPHISTICATED

üé£ Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks using personal information gathered from social media and data breaches.

üö® Warning: These emails reference real projects, colleagues, and events making them extremely convincing.
Real Example: "Hi Sarah, per our meeting yesterday about Project Phoenix, here's the budget document [malware attachment]"
BUSINESS RISK

üíº Business Email Compromise (BEC)

Attackers impersonate executives or vendors to authorize fraudulent wire transfers.

üö® Impact: Average BEC attack results in $125,000 loss. Some exceed $1 million.
Real Example: Email from "CEO" to finance: "I'm in a meeting with our lawyers. Wire $50k to this account for the acquisition. Urgent and confidential."

üõ°Ô∏è Your Defense Playbook

Practical strategies to protect yourself and your organization

1

Verify Out-of-Band

If you receive an unexpected request (especially financial), verify it through a different communication channel.

Example: Email asks you to wire money? Call the person directly using a known phone number - NOT the number in the email.
2

Enable Strong MFA

Use authenticator apps or hardware tokens instead of SMS. SMS can be intercepted through SIM swapping.

Best Options: Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, YubiKey
3

Think Before You Click

Pause when you feel urgency. That's exactly what attackers want. Take 30 seconds to verify.

Ask Yourself: Was I expecting this? Does the sender address match? Is there urgency/fear?
4

Use Password Managers

They auto-fill only on legitimate sites. If your password manager doesn't fill the form, you might be on a fake site.

Bonus: Generates unique passwords for every site, preventing credential stuffing attacks.
5

Report Suspicious Activity

Forward phishing emails to your IT/Security team and report to authorities when appropriate.

US: Report to FTC at reportphishing@apwg.org
6

Keep Systems Updated

Enable automatic updates for OS, browsers, and security software. Many attacks exploit known vulnerabilities.

Critical: Update within 24-48 hours of security patch releases.
